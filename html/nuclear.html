Nuclear reactions, a promising source of renewable energy, vary in design across the world. The United States of America mainly operates two types of reactors: pressurized water reactors and boiling water reactors.[1] But what is a nuclear reactor? A nuclear reactor takes advantage of nuclear fission, which involves collisions between neutrons and radioactive elements in a controlled environment. Certain isotopes can be unstable and release neutrons over time, a process called decay. Neutrons that are emitted from the unstable isotope, called a donor, collide with more unstable isotopes (most commonly uranium-235) to create fission products or reactionary by-products. These by-products consist of more stable elements, excess energy, heat, and more freely moving neutrons.[2] This forms a self-perpetuating cycle called nuclear fission; the new, freely moving neutrons then collide with more radioactive isotopes, creating a self-sustaining cycle of fission. This is the process by which nuclear reactors generate electrical power.



Understand A Nuclear Reactor

This is related to the variation of worldwide doctrine. All reactors operate under the same principles. A nuclear reactor contains a core composed of many components, including fuel rods, control rods, and supporting structures, to name a few. A nuclear core contains and controls the chemical reactions involved in generating nuclear energy. A nuclear reactor never completely stops during its service life. Reactivity can only be slowed down to a morsel of the total potential. A shutdown reactor has almost no reactivity. Control rods are a mechanism that are widely implemented in order to regulate reactivity. Control rods are inserted and operated to adjust reactions and the overall energy exertion of the core. These rods typically consist of elements such as a boron and cadmium. These elements are selected due to a neutron absorbing property. Typically, a core is submerged to a large volume of water. A nuclear core heats water, which creates pressure from the resulting steam. This change in pressure will power a turbine and create electricity. Water is used as a moderator and a coolant. It is a necessary component to convert heat into electricity. A moderator slows down neutrons which as a result improves reactivity. Slowing down neutrons is essential for reactivity. Fast neutrons aren’t able to split Uranium isotopes at the same efficiency. Zirconium is used in the core because it has low neutron absorption.[3] This property allows it to be viable for structurally supporting the core and having little interference with the inner reactions.





Pressurized Water Reactor

Pressure water reactors are one of the main variants that are used by the US. PWR type reactors can contain 200-300 fuel assemblies. A larger core would have between 150-250 fuel rods. This reactor vessel maintains water under extreme pressure. A notable design feature prevents circulating water from turning into steam. The water is mostly maintained in the first circuit. This system does not produce steam and serves for moderation and maintaining the core's temperature. The water in PWRs can reach temperatures of up to 325॰C. It has to be maintained at 150 atmospheres of pressure in order to prevent boiling. A second circuit will allow water to turn into steam. This system is under less pressure and contains steam. Steam is produced in order to circulate turbines.





Boiling Water Reactor

A boiling water reactor is a variant of PWRs. This type of reactor has a direct steam line connected to the core. Instead of channeling water into a pressurizer and directing it into a steam generator, this reactor eliminates these steps. This simplified design saves costs when compared to PWR reactors. A boiling water reactor simplifies the process and uses a single circuit system. Since the water around the core is always contaminated with traces of radionuclides, the turbine needs to be shielded and radiological protection provided during maintenance [4]. Generally these measures make up for the cost savings when compared to PWR reactors. Most radioactivity is very short-lived in water.





Nuclear Reactor/ Processes

Uranium is a common fuel that is used for nuclear reactors. It is used in pressurized water reactors and has been a staple in the nuclear industry. Uranium is well known for two prominent isotopes. Uranium-238 comprises most of the uranium found on the planet. A small percentage of this element contains 235 neutrons. When a neutron collides with uranium-235 many sequences occur. This isotope of uranium is very unstable and splits into fission products. It tends to produce lighter elements with additional neutrons. Further neutrons repeat this reactions with slight variation. Since U-235 nuclei do not readily absorb the high energy neutrons that are emitted during fission, it is necessary to slow the neutrons down with a "moderator" [5].




Benefits And Drawbacks

Nuclear energy is a powerful tool that can be harnessed with care. Its energy is sourced from unstable elements such as uranium. Nuclear energy is a pathway towards the future. One kilogram of uranium-235 has the potential energy output of 1.5 million kilograms of coal [6]. Unlike fossil fuels, nuclear energy does not emit greenhouse gasses. At a scale nuclear energy is astoundingly less harmful toward the environment. Nuclear energy has shown to be safe on a grand scale. As human understanding progresses this statistic improves. Nuclear energy, for example, results in 99.9% less deaths than brown coal; 99.8% fewer than coal; 99.7% fewer than oil; 97.6% fewer that gas. [7]. Nuclear reactors are considered to be inexpensive for the energy output they can provide.
