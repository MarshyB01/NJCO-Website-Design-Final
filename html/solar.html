The Earth is an astonishing 93 million miles away from the Sun, yet it takes only 8 minutes and 20 seconds for its light to reach us. Plants use their energy for growth; animals need plants to survive; without heat from the Sun, Earth would freeze; solar energy enables life on Earth to flourish. Solar energy is energy that comes from the sun. Unlimited, inexhaustible, and clean, many turn to solar energy to answer the world’s climate crisis. Humans have learned to harness this energy through the use of solar panels, an increasingly prevalent method of generating zero-emission energy. Solar panels are a revolutionary evolution in the energy sector; they help reduce our dependence on fossil fuels such as coal and oil, and they can help repurpose land that was previously used for non-renewable energy generation. Not only does solar energy currently have the power to completely end the energy crisis—much of the infrastructure required to harness it already exists, and averaged across a year, for every square meter on Earth, we receive roughly 342 watts of energy[1]—but widespread adoption of solar panels can create jobs, stimulate economic growth, and make energy cheaper for everyone around the world.[2] Developing green infrastructure enhances the resilience and sustainability of cities. Moreover, solar panels can serve as a catalyst for innovation and research in materials science, engineering, and energy storage technologies, driving advancements that benefit society as a whole.



This surge in adoption reflects a growing awareness of the need for sustainable energy sources amidst concerns over climate change and environmental degradation. However, the widespread deployment of solar panels raises questions about their inner workings, prompting a deeper exploration into the science behind their functionality.[3][4] Before examining how solar panels work, we first need to review some important concepts that are now ubiquitous in everyday life: the concepts of semiconductors (primarily silicon) and doping, a process by which impurities are purposefully introduced to semiconductors to improve conductivity. Semiconductors are the unsung hero of the modern world; not only are they fundamental to the discussion of solar panels, but they also play a major role in our everyday lives; they are fundamental components of transistors, which are electronic gates that control electrical signals, and are used in almost every electronic device today, including integrated circuits, electronic components that can perform complex tasks,[5][6] and, of course, the focus of this section, solar cells, which are electronic components used in solar panels that convert sunlight to electricity.[7]



The Semiconductor

“Semiconductor shortages,” “Silicon Valley,” phones, tablets, computers—you might have heard conversations involving the word “semiconductor” on the news. However, to many, semiconductors are confounding, foreign objects, beyond explanation. To understand what semiconductors are, we need to review some basic information regarding conductors and insulators. A conductor is any material that can easily conduct energy, or more appropriately, can freely transfer electrons. Metals are good at conducting electricity, meaning they can easily transfer electrons from one object to another. The atoms in metals form a medium through which electrons can easily enter and leave. An insulator is any object that resists the flow of electricity. The atoms of insulating materials are tightly bound, meaning electrons are unable to move freely. For example, the tightly bound electrons in insulators like rubber are not shared between neighboring atoms.



A semiconductor is an element, compound, or material that conducts electricity under specific conditions. Counterintuitively, think of a semiconductor as an element that has both the electrical conductivity of an insulator and a conductor. Because it is neither a good insulator nor a good conductor, they are useful in controlling electric current. Semiconductors have neither the tendency to gain nor lose electrons, as their valence shells tend to contain four electrons. Because the electrical conductivity of semiconductors can change with applied current, they are ideal components of photovoltaic systems.[8]



Because of its reliability, compatibility with existing processes, energy efficiency, compact potential,[9] and abundance (silicon is the second most abundant element in the earth’s crust),[10] silicon (Si) of Group XVI is the most widely used semiconductor material in the industry. It is worth noting that because the conductivity of silicon is low, it, in its purest form, is not an ideal electrical component. Silicon has 14 electrons across its three shells—two in its first, eight in its second, yet only four in its third and final shell. Its final (valence) shell is only half full. Because silicon has four valence electrons, again, with neither a tendency to gain nor lose electrons, when bonded together, they arrange themselves in a strong crystal lattice (a repeated pattern or arrangement of atoms or ions) where electrons cannot freely move, a concept inherent to its semiconducting properties.[5] Each valence electron of silicon bonds to another valence electron from its neighboring silicon, leaving no holes or excess electrons, which is one reason why pure silicon a bad conductor. And hence, doping.



Doping

Another reason why silicon is widely used in the production of semiconductors is because it can easily be doped by impurities to improve their conductivity; solar cells can’t work without doping. Similar to how people may use drugs to improve their physical performance, or rather dope, doping within the context of electronics at the atomic level is a process that entails introducing foreign elements to semiconductors to improve conductivity. Traditionally, doping occurs at around one ppm (parts per million) up to 1,000 ppm.[11] As stated in the previous section, silicon in its crystal lattice has no free electrons because it has four valence electrons, each bonding to one atom of silicon.



N-Type Doping - in n-type semiconductors, the “n” meaning “negative,” carrying a negative charge, elements with five valence electrons are introduced into the crystal lattice structure, generally phosphorus (P), arsenic (As), or antimony (Sb) of Group XV on the periodic table (though most commonly phosphorous). Phosphorus has five electrons in its outer valence shell, not four, yet is atomically similar enough to fit into its lattice structure, meaning if small amounts of it are introduced to the lattice, it can easily bond with the surrounding silicon. However, because it has five valence electrons, not four, one electron is not involved in chemical bonding. The extra electron is suspended near the phosphorus atom. Because it is not involved in bonding, it takes very little energy to make the electron leave, making it free to move throughout the lattice structure;[12] the other four are bonded to the surrounding pure silicon.[13] To the left is a visual representation of this process.



P-Type Doping - in p-type semiconductors, the “p” meaning “positive,” carrying a positive charge, elements with three valence electrons in Group XIII are introduced to the crystal lattice structure (usually boron [B], as its atomic size makes it chemically compatible with silicon, making it suitable for substitution into the silicon lattice without causing significant distortions or defects). When mixed into the silicon lattice, they form “holes” in the lattice,[14] or places where electrons are not present but should be (spots of vacancy), because, again, boron only contains three valence electrons, one less electron than is required to bond with each of its surrounding silicon atoms.[12][15] P-type semiconductors are positive because the holes they form lack an electron to fill the space.[16]



P—N Junctions, the PV Effect, and Generation

N-type and p-type semiconductors work to improve silicon’s electrical conductivity; the n-type semiconductors add electrons and the p-type semiconductors provide spaces for the electrons to fill. But what does this all mean?



Together, both types of semiconductors form areas called p-n junctions, areas of contact between the two materials. Solar cells contain a thin layer of an n-type semiconductor situated atop a thin layer of an n-type semiconductor. Areas near p-n junctions have holes from p-type semiconductors and an abundance of electrons near n-type semiconductors. Because they are attracted to each other, the extra electrons in the n-type semiconductor fill the extra holes in the p-type semiconductor, and the area near p-n junctions forms a depletion layer, a region where there are no mobile charge carriers (i.e. no holes and no extra electrons).[17] The n-type side of the depletion layer has positively charged ions, while the p-type side has negatively charged ions. The migration of charges forms an electric field across the junction, and, thus, the depletion layer effectively becomes an insulator between the two semiconducting regions, preventing electrons in the n-type layer from entering holes in the p-type layer.[12][18] Both sides maintain an equilibrium of holes and electrons.



Photoelectric cells can be most aptly described as “electrical sandwiches.” The middle “meat” layer consists of the two topical different types of semiconducting silicon. The “top bun” corresponds to metal contacts that attract photons (from solar rays) and transport electricity. The “bottom bun” corresponds to more metal contacts that are responsible for collecting the electric current generated by the movement of electrons, providing the useful electrical power generated by the solar cell.[19][20] Solar cells are then encased in glass, protecting them from physical damage.



Photons from sunlight incident on a solar cell can either be reflected, absorbed, or pass through the cell; only electrons that are absorbed by the solar cell can produce energy. When photons strike the solar cell, electrons in the n-type silicon are ejected, forming empty holes, and thinning the depletion layer. Electrons travel up from the n-type silicon, leaving a hole that travels back down through the depletion layer to the p-type silicon. If there is a path that current can run through, electrons will flow to the p-type silicon to meet holes. This movement of electrons generates current and voltage and is the process by which solar panels generate power, called the photovoltaic effect.[21] Think of this process like a battery; a solar panel has a positive and negative end, just like a battery, so electrons flow in a specific direction.



Benefits and Pitfalls

Solar energy is sustainable, renewable, and plentiful; at its most fundamental level, solar panels can generate electricity for as long as the Sun exists, which scientists estimate to be at least another 5 billion years. Because they have no moving parts, solar panels are relatively low maintenance; the only thing people need to consider when using solar panels is whether they have access to direct sunlight. Because they can be installed on nearly any surface, they are portable and can enable the generation of renewable energy in areas where it is not possible to produce energy from other sources. Solar panels can be functional for at least 20–25 years after initial installation, meaning that they can last for decades. On average, solar panels break even 6–10 years after initial installation; solar panels can lower the average monthly utility bill of homeowners, and houses entirely reliant on solar panels can operate when the grid goes down. Solar energy is safe, effectively unlimited, and not harmful to the environment, and all of the solar energy that reaches Earth is sufficient to meet all the energy needs of the population and economy of the world. [22][23]



However, solar energy production does not come without its disadvantages. Solar panels gradually lose their efficiency as temperatures rise, their optimal temperature is approximately 25 degrees Celsius; for every one-degree increase in temperature, the effectiveness of solar panels decreases by 0.5%.[24] Weather changes can affect the rate of solar energy production, and, obviously, they are unable to generate energy at night because they are entirely dependent on sunlight to function (generally, the energy output is highest in the Summer, when energy surpluses are at a maximum, and lowest in the Winter, where energy consumption is at its highest, which can attest to its seasonal limitations). However, even in ideal conditions, efficiency generally sits around 15%–20%.[25] Solar cells can only accept low-energy photons such as those from red light waves and can not efficiently process high-energy light waves.[26] To many, because of the high costs associated with initial solar investments solar panels are inaccessible. Energy production is directly proportional to the size of the panel and the amount of sunlight they have access to, meaning obstacles and shadows must be eliminated to fully take advantage of energy production. This means that, though solar panels can be attached to almost any surface, many locations are not optimal for energy production; solar farms often clear large areas, causing the loss of vegetation and, consequently, countless animal habitats. The pollution generated by solar panels, though negligible, is still linked to environmental pollution. There are environmental costs associated with the production, transportation, and installation of solar panels, albeit indirect. Solar panels are not a perfect solution to pollution generated by traditional energy production, however, they can help reduce greenhouse gas emissions and aid in the gradual shift to a more sustainable future. [22][27]
