Discovered in 1940, the Thwaites Glacier is a body of ice on the Walgreen Coast of Marie Byrd Land, a region along the coast of West Antarctica—a wall of ice standing 40 meters above the surface of the ocean, reaching over 200 meters deep.[1] Roughly the size of the state of Florida, covering approximately 192,000 square kilometers, the Thwaites Glacier is widely acclaimed for being the world’s largest glacial interface.[2]


To those unaware of its significance—simply another expanse of ice.

To scientists—a glacier with profound implications; its destabilization could lead to significant sea level rise, prompting the moniker “Doomsday Glacier.”



Annually, the Thwaites Glacier experiences a net loss of 50 billion tons of ice, and the amount of total ice loss has doubled over the past 30 years.[3] Because the glacier slopes downward to the sea, the ice loss is, for all intents and purposes, irreversible.[4] Ice shelves act as buttresses that maintain surrounding glaciers, preventing ice from flowing into the ocean; they act similar to a protective wall between glacial ice and the sea. As the Thwaites Glacier continues to melt and its ice shelves diminish, there is less preventing gravity from pulling surrounding glaciers completely into the ocean.[5] Albedo refers to the reflectivity of a surface; the higher the albedo, the more sunlight is reflected, and the less absorbed. As more ice and snow melt due to temperature increases, they expose darker surfaces underneath, such as land or ocean. Those darker surfaces have a lower albedo, meaning they absorb more sunlight than they reflect. This causes the Earth’s surface to heat up faster, which consequently accelerates the melting of ice and snow, exposing more dark surfaces, and causing more warming; this produces a self-reinforcing effect called a positive Ice–Albedo Feedback Loop.[6] [7]



Dr. Benjamin Strauss, the President and CEO of Climate Central, postulates that should the Thwaites Glacier completely decay, calamity would ensue; the United States would lose 74,600 square kilometers of land, an area currently home to 12.3 million people,[8] and globally, more than 347 million people would be displaced from their homes.[9] The collapse would also destabilize much of the West Antarctic Ice Sheet. The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) is a vital ocean current that helps distribute energy around the globe and maintain the climate by moving heat from the equator to higher altitudes. The influx of freshwater from melting ice reduces its salinity, causing it to weaken.[10] There is one culprit responsible for this threatening glacial ablation: climate change.

How Does it Affect Us Today?

According to a joint research study conducted by the University of Exeter and Stanford Doerr School of Sustainability published in December 2023, the world’s global carbon dioxide emissions reached record labels, exceeding 40 tons—of which, 37 tons were produced by fossil fuels—perpetuating a 10-year plateau.[11] The State of Global Climate 2023 Report from the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) publicized climate data, affirming broken records for greenhouse gas levels, surface temperatures, ocean heat and acidification, sea level rise, Antarctic sea ice cover, and glacier retreat.[12] According to the World Health Organization (WHO), there are already 3.6 billion people who live in areas highly vulnerable to climate change, and, between 2030 and 2050, 250,000 people are expected to die from climate change-related causes, such as malnutrition, malaria, diarrhea and heat stress per year. In the last decade, deaths from climate change-related extreme weather events in vulnerable regions, e.g. low-income countries and developing countries, were 15 times higher than in less vulnerable ones.[13] Those in marginalized communities are disproportionately affected by climate change; these communities lack the resources, infrastructure, and services necessary to address the adverse impacts of climate change.



Climate change even threatens the world’s biodiversity; at its most basic level, climate change decreases genetic diversity by causing habitat loss and changes in vegetation faster than animals can adapt to the shifts. Changes in the web of interactions are occurring at an unnaturally rapid rate. A study of 9,650 interspecific systems, or interactions between different species in a community, published in 2004 suggested that around 6,300 species were at risk of extinction.[14] Birds are particularly vulnerable to changes in climate—two studies have calculated that 0.3% of the world’s 8,750 species of land birds could go extinct by 2100,[15] and up to 30% of the 8,400 land bird species in the Western Hemisphere could go extinct.[16] Studies have also suggested climate change could cause the potential loss of 56,000 native plants and 3,700 native vertebrate species.[17] These changes are not specific to land species; oceans can absorb CO2 from the atmosphere, reducing the pH of the water over time, which can impact marine organisms with shells or exoskeletons. Animals that produce calcium carbonate structures, such as sea snails, corals, and oysters, have to expend more energy repairing damaged shells or thickening them.[18] Throughout the sea and land, we are all affected by climate change.

The Science Behind it

Global warming, a harmful phenomenon linked to destructive human activities, mainly the emissions of greenhouse gasses, fundamentally alters Earth's climate system and the many ecosystems within. Greenhouse gasses, such as carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), water vapor, and other gasses, are some of the major components that make up greenhouse emissions.[19] These gasses can absorb and re-emit infrared radiation, trapping heat within the Earth's atmosphere. While the natural greenhouse effect is essential for maintaining a constant habitable climate, continued human activities have significantly intensified its effects, through burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and continued industrial processes. These gasses can remain in the Earth's atmosphere ranging from a few years to thousands of years. Continued absorption of these gasses results in the thickening of the Earth's atmospheric blanket, causing the gasses to gradually incorporate into the atmosphere.[20] As a result, radiation from the Sun can travel through the atmosphere toward Earth's surface, however, it is not able to be reflected outwards. Rather, this radiation is trapped due to the thick atmosphere, slowly increasing the Earth's temperature.



Although only 0.04% of Earth's atmosphere contains greenhouse gases, the effects of the increased heat can be detrimental to Earth's climate. Evidence suggests that, with continued technological and industrial uses within the century, greenhouse emissions in the atmosphere will likely double. Since the passing of the Second Industrial Revolution in the late 1800s, temperature globally has increased roughly 1.7 degrees Celsius, with an estimated rate of 0.15–0.20 degree increase per decade—a seemingly inconsequential figure, yet one that frightens scientists. This two-degree increase in temperature is significant because of the heat required to heat the Earth’s surface. Proof of how such little temperature change can significantly affect the Earth can be dated throughout a period called The Little Ice Age when temperatures dropped by an estimated 2 degrees Celsius. However, to understand global warming, it is best to define and understand the current makeup of the Earth's atmosphere. It is also essential to conceptualize how these greenhouse gases react with the atmosphere and trap heat.



The Earth's atmosphere can be divided into five distinct layers, each with a unique chemical composition and characteristics that protect the Earth: the troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere, and exosphere. The effects of greenhouse gases mainly occur within the troposphere and stratosphere, where gases are filtered and accumulated. The troposphere is the lowest or first layer of Earth's atmosphere. Located roughly 10 kilometers above sea level, the troposphere extends upwards from the Earth’s surface. This layer is estimated to contain 75%–80% of the atmosphere’s total mass. The troposphere consists of roughly 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, 0.4% water vapor, and 0.04% greenhouse gases. The middle of the troposphere acts as a filter for the many pollutants that are expelled from Earth's surface.[21] Mainly through dissolution and chemical reactions, pollutants such as sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide react with other compounds suspended in the atmosphere and are often cleansed through precipitation and further filtered through water vapors. Some pollutants also undergo photolysis, a process in which sunlight breaks down the molecules, removing them from the atmosphere.[22] Yet despite the atmosphere's natural filter, pollutants such as greenhouse gases may remain within the layer longer due to specific chemical properties or circulation patterns.



This trapping of greenhouse gases often occurs on the top of the troposphere, near the stratosphere known as the ozone layer, of the chemical formula O3; the main contributor to global warming. The stratosphere, the second layer of Earth's atmosphere has an average altitude of 50 kilometers. Because it is mainly composed of nitrogen and oxygen, it acts as a barrier from invading ultraviolet radiation from the sun, absorbing energy. Unlike the troposphere, due to its high altitude and temperature, the stratosphere contains little moisture and water vapor, rendering it unable to provide the same filter. As a result, holes in the ozone can occur due to certain pollutants and chemical reactions. With the lack of convection in the stratosphere, greenhouse gases can ultimately create "holes" in the ozone.[23] This depletion of the ozone layer has detrimental effects on Earth's climate and poses serious health issues for humans, increasing exposure to ultraviolet radiation and heat.



Chlorofluorocarbon (CFC) is a common form of greenhouse gas, composed of chemicals containing atoms of carbon, chlorine, and fluorine; they are commonly found in the production of aerosol propellants. Its chemical formula is CCl3F.[24] Gaseous CFCs are highly destructive to the earth’s ozone layer; when they slowly rise into the stratosphere, they are broken down by ultraviolet radiation from the Sun, releasing chlorine atoms, which subsequently react with the ozone layer.[25] Below describes the process by which CFCs contribute to ozone depletion:




CFCs + UV Radiation → Chlorine Radicals + Ozone




After CFCs are exposed to ultraviolet radiation releasing chlorine atoms, they react with the ozone layer (again, of the chemical formula O3), to form chlorine monoxide (ClO) and oxygen (O), a process represented in the chemical equation below:




Cl + O3 → ClO + O2




When another chlorine monoxide molecule comes into contact with an oxygen atom, it breaks, releasing chlorine and oxygen, represented in the chemical equation below:




Cl + O → Cl + O2




This forms a self-perpetuating cycle that endlessly ravages the Earth’s ozone layer.[26]
